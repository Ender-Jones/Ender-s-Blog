Regression Model Evaluation Metrics: MAE, MSE, RMSE, and R² Explained

Understand the differences, advantages, and limitations of MAE, MSE, RMSE, and R² — and choose the right metric for your machine learning projects.

§01Introduction: The Essence of Regression Models

When we talk about prediction models, we're typically referring to one of two main types:

–

Regression models: continuous outputs — house prices, temperature, stock values.

–

Classification models: categorical or binary outputs — yes/no, spam/not spam, image classes.

Regression models attempt to find relationships between variables to predict continuous numeric values. But how do we know if our regression model is any good? This is where evaluation metrics come in.

§02Theoretical Foundation of Evaluation Metrics

Residuals: The Building Blocks

At the core of regression evaluation is the concept of residuals — the differences between predicted values and actual observed values:

Residual = yᵢ − ŷᵢ

Where yᵢ is the actual value and ŷᵢ is the predicted value.

Bias and Error

Bias represents systematic errors in predictions. Ideally, the sum of residuals should be close to zero, indicating that your model isn't consistently over or under-predicting values.

The Modeling Cycle

1 build→2 evaluate→3 improve→repeat until satisfactory

§04Basic Evaluation Metrics

Mean Absolute Error (MAE)

Mean Absolute Error measures the average magnitude of errors without considering their direction.

MAE = (1/n) Σ |yᵢ − ŷᵢ|

Advantages

–

Intuitive: directly represents the average error magnitude, in the original unit.

–

Robust to outliers: doesn't amplify their effect.

Limitations

–

Equal weighting: assumes all error magnitudes matter equally.

–

Optimization: not easily differentiable at zero; scale-dependent across datasets.

INFO

MAE is like a ruler that measures the average distance between predictions and reality, without caring about the direction of the error.

MSE and RMSE

MSE calculates the average of squared differences between predicted and actual values. RMSE brings the metric back to the original units.

MSE = (1/n) Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
RMSE = √MSE

Advantages — penalizes larger errors via the squared term; differentiable, so easier to optimize; minimizing MSE leads to centered predictions. Limitations — heavily influenced by outliers; MSE is not in original units (RMSE solves this); cannot directly compare across datasets.

INFO

If MAE is a ruler, then MSE is a magnifying glass that emphasizes larger errors. RMSE converts that magnified view back to the original scale.

Coefficient of Determination (R²)

R² represents the proportion of variance in the dependent variable that can be explained by the independent variables.

R² = 1 − RSS / TSS

Where RSS is the residual sum of squares Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² and TSS the total sum of squares Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)². An R² of 0.75 means the model explains 75% of the variance.

Advantages — scale-independent, so comparable across targets; intuitive as "percent of variance explained"; the standard reporting metric. Limitations — always increases with additional features (even irrelevant ones); no penalty for complexity (unlike adjusted R²); misleading if model assumptions are violated; not ideal for time series.

INFO

R² is like a school grade for your model — it tells you what percentage of the test your model got right, but doesn't tell you where it made mistakes.

§05Advanced Evaluation Metrics

Percentage-Based Errors

MAPE measures the percentage difference between actual and predicted values. Business-friendly and scale-independent — but undefined when actual values are zero, and it penalizes over-forecasting more than under-forecasting.

MAPE = (1/n) Σ |(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)/yᵢ| × 100%

SMAPE addresses MAPE's asymmetry: bounded to [0%, 200%], more balanced between over- and under-prediction, and better with zeros — though still problematic when both values are zero.

SMAPE = (1/n) Σ |(yᵢ − ŷᵢ) / ((yᵢ+ŷᵢ)/2)| × 100%

Logarithmic Error Metrics

MSLE applies a logarithmic transformation before calculating squared error; RMSLE is its square root.

MSLE = (1/n) Σ (log(yᵢ+1) − log(ŷᵢ+1))²

Particularly useful for data with exponential growth patterns, for penalizing errors at lower values more, and for datasets spanning a wide range.

Information Criteria: AIC and BIC

These balance model fit against complexity — penalizing models with more parameters to prevent overfitting. They cannot be compared across datasets.

AIC = 2k − 2ln(L)BIC = ln(n)k − 2ln(L)

Where k is the number of parameters, L the likelihood, n the number of observations.

§06How to Choose the Right Metric?

01

What is the impact of outliers? Heavily penalize them → MSE/RMSE. Don't let them dominate → MAE.

02

Is over- or under-prediction more costly? Equally important → RMSE or MAE. One direction costs more → asymmetric measures or custom loss.

03

Relative or absolute? Comparing across datasets → R², MAPE. Interpreting in original units → RMSE, MAE.

In practice: optimize one primary metric during training, evaluate with several, respect domain conventions, and know each metric's blind spots in your context.

§07Conclusion: Best Practices

–

MAE — average error magnitude, plainly.

–

MSE / RMSE — optimization, and when large errors matter most.

–

R² — explained variance, model comparison.

–

MAPE / SMAPE — business contexts needing percentages.

–

MSLE / RMSLE — exponential data, relative errors.

The ultimate goal isn't to optimize metrics but to build models that generalize to unseen data — and to understand what your data is telling you.

TIP

In regression modeling, metrics are your compass, guiding you toward better predictions and deeper understanding of your data's story.
